TEESTA RIVER

Description

Teesta River originates from the Tso Lhamo Lake at an elevation of about 5280 m in the North Sikkim district. It is the right bank major tributary of the Brahmaputra river system. Like many other major Himalayan rivers of India, like the Ganga, Teesta is a glacier-fed river. Pahurni glacier, Khangse glacier and ChhoLhamo Lake are also considered the sources of the Teesta River.



History

Teesta River is a 414 km (257 mi) long river that rises in the Pauhunri Mountain of eastern Himalayas, flows through the Indian states of Sikkim and West Bengal and subsequently enters Bangladesh through Rangpur division. In Bangladesh, it merges with Brahmaputra River which after meeting some other major rivers of the Bengal delta finally falls into the Bay of Bengal.It drains an area of 12,540 km2 (4,840 sq mi).In India, the Teesta flows through Mangan District, Gangtok District, Pakyong District, Kalimpong district, Darjeeling District, Jalpaiguri District, Cooch Behar districts and the cities of Rangpo, Jalpaiguri and Mekhliganj. In Bangladesh, it flows through Lalmonirhat District, Rangpur District, Kurigram District and Gaibandha District. It joins the Brahmaputra River at Phulchhari Upazila in Bangladesh. 305 km (190 mi) of the river lies in India and 109 km (68 mi) in Bangladesh. The Teesta is the largest river of Sikkim and second largest river of West Bengal after the Ganges.



Religious Significance

Mother Teesta: The Remover of Diseases:The Teesta River is also worshiped by the Hindu Napalese. They call the river goddess TeestaMaai, literally Mother Teesta. Sarat Chandra Mitra (1931) informs us about the worship of Teesta Maai in Jalpaiguri. While staying in Jalpaiguri, he witnessed the worship of the river goddess by a Nepalese servant. The servant, as shown by Mitra, was ill, and when he recovered from illness, he took a vow to worship the goddess as a gesture of gratitude (p. 209). Charu Chandra Sanyal (1965) also opined that Teesta Budi worship is done by the Rajbanshis to remove evil spirits that may cause diseases (p. 145).One of the most important reasons for the worship of Teesta Budi, as shown by Girija Shankar Roy (1975), is the warding off of diseases (p. 102). Citing examples from different parts of Lower Bengal, Mitra (1931) argued that similar traditions of worship where people often take vows to do something in return for a favor from the deity are a common practice (p. 210). Every year on Poush Sankranti, people assemble at Beni or Tribeni, 72 kilometers from Darjeeling, to propitiate Teesta Maai by making animal sacrifices (De., 598). The place is considered sacred as a Sangam, or conjunction, where the rivers Teesta and Rangeet met. And as argued by Gupta (1984), the Hindu scriptures advocate bathing in the Sangamas it leads to salvation, and the people around the hills as well as the plains came here to achieve salvation.



RIVER BASIN

The Teesta River Basin is situated in the northeastern part of India, primarily in the state of Sikkim, as well as parts of West Bengal and the Indian-administered territory of the state of West Bengal. The Teesta River originates in the Himalayas and flows through the states of Sikkim and West Bengal before entering Bangladesh, where it eventually merges with the Brahmaputra River.
