MANAS RIVER

DISCRIPTION

The Manas River, which flows between Bhutan and India in the Himalayan foothills, is transboundary.It is the largest of Bhutan's four major river systems; the others are the Amo Chu or Torsa River, the Wong Chu or Raidak River, and the Puna Tshang Chu or Sankosh River. Before debouching into India in western Assam, it is met by three other major streams. The river runs for 400 kilometres, passing through Tibet, China for 24 kilometres, Bhutan for 272 kilometres, and Assam for 104 kilometres before joining the mighty Brahmaputra River at Jogighopa. The Aie River, a major tributary of the Manas, joins it in Assam at Bangpari.



History

The geological formation of the Manas River dates back to antiquity, as the convergence of numerous tributaries in the Tien Shan mountains gives rise to its majestic flow. Over millennia, the river has carved its path through the rugged terrain, shaping the landscape and providing a lifeline for the inhabitants of the region. In ancient times, the Manas River served as a vital artery of trade and transportation, connecting disparate communities and fostering cultural exchange. Along its banks, settlements flourished, drawing sustenance from the fertile plains irrigated by its waters. Archaeological evidence attests to the presence of ancient civilizations that thrived in the Manas River basin, leaving behind a legacy of artifacts and monuments that testify to their ingenuity and resilience. Throughout history, the Manas River has also played a strategic role in the geopolitical dynamics of the region. As empires rose and fell, control over the river and its resources became a coveted prize, with competing powers vying for dominance. From the conquests of ancient conquerors to the imperial ambitions of colonial powers, the Manas River bore witness to the ebb and flow of human history. In the modern era, the Manas River continued to shape the destinies of the nations through which it flows. With the advent of industrialization and urbanization, the river's waters became increasingly exploited for agricultural, industrial, and domestic purposes. Large-scale irrigation projects were undertaken to harness its potential, leading to profound alterations in its flow regime and ecological balance. The legacy of the Manas River is also enshrined in the cultural heritage of the peoples who have called its banks home. For the Kyrgyz people, the river holds deep symbolic significance, evoking tales of heroism, resilience, and unity. The epic of Manas, a cornerstone of Kyrgyz folklore, celebrates the river as a source of inspiration and a testament to the indomitable spirit of the human soul.



Religious Significance

The Manas River, flowing through the majestic landscapes of the Tien Shan mountains, has inspired myths, legends, and religious tales among the peoples of Central Asia. In the ancient folklore of the Kyrgyz people, the river is often associated with the epic of Manas, a legendary hero whose exploits are celebrated in oral tradition. According to myth, the Manas River served as a boundary between the mortal realm and the realm of spirits, embodying the liminal space where the earthly and the divine converge
Like many rivers revered in religious traditions, the Manas River holds significance as a purifying agent, capable of cleansing the soul and renewing the spirit. Rituals of ablution and immersion in the river's waters are common practices among the faithful, symbolizing a symbolic washing away of sin and impurity. For pilgrims and devotees, a visit to the Manas River represents an opportunity for spiritual rejuvenation and communion with the divine.
Along the banks of the Manas River, numerous sacred sites and shrines attest to its religious significance and the reverence accorded to it by the faithful. Temples, monasteries, and holy wells dot the landscape, serving as places of pilgrimage and worship for believers seeking blessings and divine favor. These sacred spaces are imbued with a sense of sanctity and awe, drawing devotees from far and wide to pay homage to the river and the deities associated with it.

Festivals and Ceremonies

Throughout the year, the Manas River becomes the focal point of religious festivals and ceremonies that celebrate its significance in the spiritual life of the community. From elaborate processions to solemn rituals of offering and prayer, these observances serve to honor the river and invoke its blessings for the well-being of the people and the land. Among the most revered festivals is the annual Manas Day, a joyous occasion marked by feasting, music, and dance, as the faithful gather to express their gratitude and devotion to the river and its divine guardians.

RIVER BASIN

The Manas is the biggest river basin, which drains almost all the catchments of Central and Eastern Bhutan. It comprises of four major sub-basins, namely: Mangde Chhu and Chamkhar Chhu (which both originate close to Gangkhar Puensum), Kuri Chhu (which originates from China), and Dangmechhu (formed by joining two main tributariesKholongchhu that originates from the northern-eastern part of Trashi Yangtse and Gongri that originates from China and flows from Aurunacha Pradesh in India to Bhutan). Kurichhu and Gongri are transboundray Rivers that originates from Autonomous region of Tibet, China. The three minor rivers that drains through the southeastern corner of the country are Nyera Ama Ri, Nonori Chhu, and Jomo Chhu (Dhansari). There are fourteen manual stations ( Nine principle and five secondary) in the Manas basin. The catchment delineation is based on the nine principle stations located at Bjizam and Tingtibi on Mangdechu, Kurjey and Bemethang on Chamkharchu, Sumpa and Kurizampa on Kurichu, Muktirap on Kholongchu, Uzorong on Gomgri and Panbang on Drangmechu.



IRRIGATION PROJECTS

Soviet Era Irrigation Projects:

During the Soviet period, extensive irrigation projects were undertaken in Central Asia, including areas within the Manas River basin. These projects aimed to transform the arid landscapes into productive agricultural regions, primarily for cotton cultivation. Large-scale canal networks were constructed to divert water from rivers like the Manas to irrigate vast tracts of land.

Manas-Canal System:

The Manas-Canal System is a network of irrigation canals and channels that divert water from the Manas River and its tributaries to irrigate agricultural land in Kyrgyzstan and Kazakhstan. These canals play a crucial role in supporting agricultural production, particularly in areas where water availability is limited.



ISSUES

he Manas River basin is vulnerable to the impacts of climate change, including changes in precipitation patterns, increased frequency of extreme weather events, and melting glaciers in the Tien Shan mountains. These changes can exacerbate water scarcity, alter ecosystem dynamics, and affect the availability of water for irrigation and hydropower generation.
